Seximal (Base-6 Numbering System)

You're probably familiar with the :decimal system.

But, did you know that there is a better way to count?

This is the :seximal system.

So, What's Wrong With Decimal?

You've probably noticed this, but fractions in decimal are really inconvienent.

Quick, off the top of your head: what's 1/7th? What's 1/3rd? What's 1/9th?

You probably couldn't answer the first one, you could answer the second one, and maybe the third one. If you're a nerd.

:The answers, by the way.

:Those same fractions, in seximal.

What's wrong with that? After all, don't we have ten :fingers?

But in the decimal system, we don't take advantage of this. We use the whole both hands to display only one digit.

Most people have two hands, with five fingers each. Why does this matter?

It's really inefficient. If we treated each finger as a binary bit, we could get up to 2^10 = 1024 on two hands.

While this may be best for computers, when you need to show a number to someone using your hands, which is the most likely reason to count on your fingers, this can get really confusing.

So really, think about it. Do we really have ten fingers? Or do we have two hands, of five fingers each?

With two hands, we have an evolutionarily-gifted setup, perfect for counting of two base six digits, allowing counting all the way up to 35, (or 55 in seximal).

In fact, the NBA has long used base six for basketball jerseys, with the specific purpose of being easy to show on two hands. They sadly no longer do this.

Psychology + Numbers

Psychology likes the number six.

No, but, for real.

Numbers in base six require 29% (decimal) more digits to express. However, that's a small price to pay for the 40% (decimal) reduction of the amount of digits!

We also already use base twelve and sixty for timekeeping. (24 hours in a day, 60 seconds in a minute, etc.). If we switched to a seximal system, we would end up with 40 hours in a day, 50 days in a month, and 20 months in a year.

Still not convinced?

Computers can easily be based on six-state logic by combining the traditional two-state logic with the not-as-common-but-still-easy three state logic.

Six is :hypercomposite. The next hypercomposite is 12, which is just 6*2.

(If you're wondering, this is the reason fractions are so easy)

(The other reason is that in any base N, the fractions 1/N-1 and 1/N+1 have very easy repeating decimals.)

:toki pona already has widely accepted unofficial names for the numbers 0-5. (ala, wan, tu, san, po, luka).

Most currency systems around the world are unevenly spaced. They have a 1 bill, a 10 bill, a 100 bill, etc. but they usually also have in-betweens. A 5 bill, a 50 bill.

This is because it is inconvienent to count up 10 1-bills before switching denominations. In a base six system, each new denomination is just 6 times the last. 6 is a very manageable number.
